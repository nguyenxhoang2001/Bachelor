4.3 Instance generation and parameterization
All instances are generated synthetically using a controlled pseudo-random procedure.
Structural parameters such as the number of tasks, number of bays, and number of
cranes are fixed according to the experimental design. Random seeds are stored to
ensure reproducibility .
Each task i is assigned a bay position li drawn uniformly from {1, . . . ,num_bays}. Pro-
cessing times pi are independently sampled from a continuous uniform distribution on
the interval [3, 180].
Precedence relations are generated locally within each bay . For each ordered pair of
tasks located in the same bay , a precedence constraint is introduced with probability
0.3. No precedence relations are created across different bays.
The interference set Ψ is defined based on spatial adjacency . Two tasks interfere if and
only if they are located in adjacent bays, i.e., |li − lj| = 1. This models safety distance
constraints between cranes operating in neighboring bays.
Travel times are proportional to the absolute difference in bay indices, with one time
unit per bay difference. Quay cranes are initially positioned evenly along the vessel
and return to their initial positions after completing their assigned tasks. All cranes are
available at time zero.
The objective function consists of a weighted combination of performance components
with parameters α1 = 100 and α2 = 1 . The chosen weights prioritize the primary

For T = 5, all instances are solved to proven optimality within negligible runtime for

A substantial decrease in performance is observed when increasing the number of tasks
to T = 10. For both crane configurations, none of the five instances can be solved to
proven optimality within the time limit of 1000 seconds. All runs terminate due to the
imposed time limit, and the average optimality gap increases sharply to 88.68% for
K = 2 and 70.72% for K = 3.
For T ≥ 15, this pattern persists. All instances reach the time limit and the final optimal-
ity gaps remain at very high levels. The task scaling experiment thus reveals a distinct
transition between T = 5 and T = 10, where Gurobi ceases to provide proven optimal
solutions within the fixed computational budget.
4.4.2 Capacity test
The capacity test refines the analysis of the observed transition region between T = 5
and T = 10. To determine this boundary more precisely , the number of tasks is increased
incrementally between these values under a fixed bay layout of 30. For each task size
and crane configuration, five independent instances are generated and solved with a
time limit of 1000 seconds. The proportion of instances solved to proven optimality is
recorded.

4.4.3 Time limit experiment
The time limit experiment examines the influence of available computation time on
solution quality and bound progression. A fixed configuration with K = 3, |Ω| = 10,
and num_bays = 10 is considered. For each of five random seeds, identical instances are
solved under increasing time limits. The final optimality gap, the incumbent objective
value (primal bound), and the best bound (dual bound) are recorded at termination.
Figure 4 shows that the optimality gap decreases as the time limit increases. The re-
duction is systematic across all instances. However, the improvement rate is moderate.
Although the gap is reduced when extending the time limit from 1000 s to 2500 s, none
of the instances reaches proven optimality within the considered time horizon. The
decrease in gap becomes progressively smaller as runtime increases, suggesting dimin-
ishing marginal returns of additional computational effort.
